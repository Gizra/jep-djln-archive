Seeing is Believing: The Impact of a Blended Learning Immersion

April 03, 2014 | By Rabbi Hersh Hiller

Rabbi Hersh Hiller is a Judaic Studies Teacher at Torah Day School of Seattle, WA and was one of the participants on our two-day immersion into blended learning in October of 2013, put together by DJLN and The Samis Foundation.  Together we hosted 11 educators from six Seattle Jewish day schools on a real Jewish Education Experience. During our time together, we visited five different Jewish schools in the New York area to learn how they integrate technology and blended models to support student learning.

Fast-forward to March 2014, where we bump into Hersh at the iJED Conference and we got to talking about our time together back in October. The trip made such an impression on Rabbi Hiller that we have to share it with you.

To find him online, visit his website at www.rabbihiller.weebly.com and tweet him at @ rabbihiller

DJLN: What were your impressions of online and blended learning before and after your participation in our deep dive back October of 2013?

HH: Before my trip, all the research that I had done was only in print, and I had never actually seen blended learning in practice. It was very hard to visualize it. After the trip, my impressions from seeing it in practice and in use is that it definitely has a place in education. Meaning, it is a very useful tool that I think that both teachers and students can learn from.

DJLN: How has your own teaching practice changed since the trip?

HH: My own teaching practice has changed quite a bit. There are different degrees of blended learning, and I rely more heavily upon it since our time together. I encourage my students to use the digital content as opposed to my own content, and then incorporate it into the lesson.

DJLN: Can you give use some examples of what this looks like in practice?

HH: This could mean I assign them a tutorial on using a program, it could mean using an application for the content covered in class, or it could mean a review activity using an online application.

DJLN: What have been the main challenges you’ve encountered in implementing more online and blended learning in your classroom?

HH: The main challenge is the infrastructure or lack thereof in my school. For instance, we have a lot of connectivity issues. It’s by far my most limiting challenge. Beyond that, the sky’s the limit.

DJLN:  How did our trip together inform the way you experienced the iJED conference?

HH: October was really the introduction and exposure, and iJED was really the application. In October, we got a snapshot of technology in different types of schools in different ways. It helped me establish a perspective in broad strokes. The conference helped me put it into practice and establish application. If I had gone to the conference without the October trip, I would have been completely overwhelmed and not known what to look at first. The conference was the next step in professional development.

DJLN: What stuck out most for you from the conference?

HH: I think the thing that stuck out most was the usage of space and time. Even break time at the conference was used for learning. It was different kind of learning, such as simple conversation or tutorials, but still learning. That was the huge difference in this conference.

Furthermore, whenever I wanted to know what was going on, I looked at my device. The use of Twitter and other tools took interactions deeper, removing the distance between facilitators and attendees.

DJLN: How did our trip in October influence your Twitter use?

HH: Before October, I couldn’t understand what Twitter was for and I never got into it. I decided to give it a shot, and within 5 minutes I had 4 retweets and I thought ‘This really works!’ Shortly afterwards, I listened to an edtech podcast and tweeted to the guest, and we had a back and forth on Twitter and that created a professional relationship. “I was like ‘That’s crazy, that would have never happened with an email!’” There were so many obstacles that were passed over with a simple 140 character tweet. I think for me that was the most amazing part of it. Even now, I’m getting different kinds of relationships using Twitter at the conference.

DJLN: Thank you so much for taking the time to speak with us! Any last thoughts before we say goodbye?

HH: Only that I owe a tremendous amount of gratitude to SAMIS and DJLN for providing me with the opportunity to learn at all those amazing schools back in October.
